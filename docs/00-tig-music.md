Here's a practical toolkit organized by phase of the writing process, weighted toward your context — guitar/keys, worship-oriented lyrics, and blues-influenced harmony.
Lyrics

RhymeZone (free, web) — the standard quick-reference for rhymes, near rhymes, and synonyms. Best used late in a draft: draft the section naturally, then search only the words that feel weak or predictable, since using rhyme tools too early can make songs sound engineered rather than spoken. Drumloop AI
MasterWriter (paid) — a full lyric workstation with rhyming dictionaries, phrase libraries, and word families. It's a lyric specialist, not an all-in-one songwriting app, but it's the deepest word-craft tool available and well suited to lyric-driven writing like worship music. Drumloop AI
Songwriter's Pad — organizes lyrics by song section (verse, chorus, bridge) with a rhyme finder and audio recording, and its section-based structure mirrors how many writers think. Lazyjot
BibleGateway / Blue Letter Bible — for scripture-rooted lyrics, keyword and cross-reference searching across translations is invaluable. Since you've already standardized on the WEB translation for public-domain use, that pairing carries into lyric quotation cleanly.

Chords, melody, and theory

Hookpad (Hooktheory) — a browser-based, theory-aware harmony sketchpad for dragging chords and melodies around in key; strong for testing choruses and trying borrowed chords, inversions, and alternate harmonic routes. Given your Nashville Number System familiarity, this will feel natural, and it's excellent for exploring the bVII, IV/I, and dominant-7 colors common in blues-inflected worship. Drumloop AI
Song Cage (newer entrant) — built around guitar fretboard diagrams with real voicings and capo adjustment, a context-aware chord suggestion engine, and lyrics living alongside the chord lane — explicitly covering CAGED and the Nashville Number System. Worth a look as an all-in-one. Song Cage
Moises — you already use it; its chord detection and stem separation remain one of the best ways to reverse-engineer harmonic ideas from reference tracks.

Capture and demo

Voice Memos + Notes — still the zero-friction baseline. On recent iPhones, Layered Recordings even lets you sing a vocal over an existing instrumental without headphones. (Your Honor Magic V3 equivalent: any quick recorder with cloud sync.) Song Cage
GarageBand — you have it on the Mac mini; with the iRig HD2 and AmpliTube it's fully sufficient for writing demos. No need to add a DAW at this stage.
BandLab (free, cloud) — useful specifically if you co-write remotely, since collaborators can open the same session in a browser.

Worship-specific

CCLI SongSelect — since you hold a CCLI account, SongSelect gives you chord charts and lead sheets of the worship canon in any key, which doubles as a study library for song structure and lyric conventions in the genre.
Planning Center Services — already on your radar; relevant once songs move from writing to team rehearsal (keys, arrangements, charts).

A pragmatic starting stack for you: RhymeZone + a scripture search tool for lyrics, Hookpad for harmonic sketching, GarageBand for demos, and MuseScore (which you already use) when a chart needs formalizing. That covers the full lyric-to-lead-sheet pipeline with only one or two new tools. As one review put it, most working songwriters use a combination of tools, and the best software is whatever removes the most friction from your specific process. Lazyjot
One caution on the AI-generation category (Suno, Udio, LyricStudio): these are fine for ideation, but for a tribute album or songs intended for congregational or commercial release, provenance and copyright ownership of AI-generated material remain unsettled — keep the core melody and lyric authorship human if distribution through RouteNote/DistroKid is the goal.
